\begin{abstract}
We develop an analytic framework---\emph{Quantum Stabilizer Entropy} (QSE)---for
computing the bipartite von Neumann entropy of pure states produced by
\emph{unidirectional} CNOT circuits acting on product input states.
Central to the framework is \textbf{Theorem~14 (T14)}, which expresses
$\VNE(B)$ as the Shannon entropy of a probability distribution computed by a
Walsh--Hadamard transform over $\F_2$.  The key novelty is the
\emph{continuous} dependence on rotation angles $\theta_i$: for pure
stabilizer states ($\theta_i = \pi/2$) the entropy collapses to the
$\F_2$-rank of the connectivity matrix $M$, recovering the known rank formula;
T14 extends this to the full continuous-parameter regime.

From T14 we derive:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[(T-RANK)] $\VNE(B) \leq \rk(M)$ for all rotation angles;
  \item[(T-OPT)]  equality at $\theta_i = \pi/2$;
  \item[(T15A)]   multi-layer composition collapses to $M_{\text{eff}} = \bigoplus_k M_k$
                  \emph{(unidirectional layers only)};
  \item[(T17)]    extension to arbitrary pure states on subsystem $A$;
  \item[(T18)]    period-2 entropy oscillation under repeated application;
  \item[(T20)]    a bidirectional CNOT circuit satisfying $U^3 = I$ with
                  period-3 entropy (numerically verified).
\end{enumerate}
All results are verified against exact state-vector simulation;
the accompanying open-source library reproduces every theorem in under
15 seconds on commodity hardware.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Characterizing how entanglement depends on circuit structure and parameters is
a central problem in quantum information theory.  For Clifford (stabilizer)
circuits, entanglement properties are well understood and often reducible to
linear-algebraic quantities such as rank over $\F_2$~\cite{audenaert2005entanglement,fattal2004entanglement}.
In contrast, obtaining analytic expressions for entanglement in parameterized
quantum circuits with continuous gate parameters remains more limited.

In this work, we consider a restricted class of circuits consisting of product
input states acted on by layers of unidirectional CNOT gates.  Within this
setting, we derive an explicit expression for the bipartite von Neumann entropy
of a subsystem (Theorem~\ref{thm:t14}).  The resulting reduced density matrix
is diagonal in the computational basis, and its spectrum can be written in
terms of a Walsh--Hadamard transform over $\F_2$.  This leads to a closed-form
entropy expression with explicit dependence on the input rotation angles.

Our results recover known rank-based bounds from stabilizer theory in special
cases, while providing a continuous interpolation away from these points.  We
also discuss structural properties of the circuit class, including entropy
bounds in terms of $\F_2$ rank and simple composition rules for multilayer
circuits under suitable assumptions.

\paragraph{Scope and limitations.}
Our analysis applies to circuits consisting of unidirectional CNOT layers
($A \to B$) acting on product inputs, with subsystem $B$ initialized in
$\ket{0}^{\otimes n_B}$.  Circuits involving bidirectional interactions,
additional Clifford gates on $B$, or entangled inputs on $B$ fall outside this
framework.  Within this restricted regime, however, the formulas offer a
tractable way to study parameter-dependent entanglement.  All statements are
supported by numerical verification to high precision.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
The main contribution is Theorem~\ref{thm:t14}, which provides a closed-form
Walsh--Hadamard expression for the bipartite von Neumann entropy in a class of
parameterized CNOT circuits.  Secondary contributions include rank-based
entropy bounds (T-RANK, T-OPT), a multi-layer composition rule (T15A), and
structural results on circuit dynamics (T18, T20).

\paragraph{Relation to prior work.}
Our results build on the stabilizer formalism, where entanglement is
characterized by algebraic invariants of binary
matrices~\cite{audenaert2005entanglement,fattal2004entanglement}.  Unlike
these discrete results, our formula provides a closed-form expression for the
continuous dependence of entropy on rotation parameters.  The use of
Walsh--Hadamard transforms is closely related to Fourier analysis over
$\F_2^n$, which has appeared in studies of stabilizer entropy and related
quantities~\cite{bataille2022cnot,goodenough2024noisy}.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Entanglement of stabilizer states.}
The connection between $\F_2$ linear algebra and bipartite entanglement entropy
is well established for \emph{stabilizer states}.  Fattal et
al.~\cite{fattal2004entanglement} showed that the entanglement entropy of a
stabilizer state across any bipartition equals the $\F_2$-rank of a submatrix
of the stabilizer generators.  Audenaert and
Plenio~\cite{audenaert2005entanglement} proved that every bipartite mixed
stabilizer state is locally equivalent to a direct product of maximally
entangled pairs and a separable state, with all entanglement measures
efficiently computable from the stabilizer group.  These results establish the
rank formula $\VNE = \rk(M)$ at $\theta = \pi/2$, which T-OPT recovers as a
special case.  The contribution of T14 is to extend this to the
\emph{continuous-parameter} regime: for $\theta \neq \pi/2$ the state is no
longer a stabilizer state, and the rank formula no longer applies.  T14
provides the exact entropy for all $\theta$ via a Walsh--Hadamard transform.

\paragraph{CNOT circuit structure and entanglement.}
Patel, Markov, and Hayes~\cite{patel2008optimal} studied the synthesis of
linear reversible circuits (CNOT-only) over $\F_2$, establishing the
connection between CNOT circuit composition and $\F_2$ matrix multiplication.
Bataille~\cite{bataille2022cnot} studied entanglement creation by CNOT
circuits acting on factorized states, characterising the group structure of
CNOT circuits and the conditions under which different entanglement classes
arise.  Our T15A (XOR composition of unidirectional layers) is consistent with
this picture: the effective matrix $M_{\mathrm{eff}} = \bigoplus_k M_k$ is
precisely the $\F_2$ sum of the layer matrices, and the period-2 corollary
(T18) follows from CNOT self-inverseness.  Bataille's work focuses on the
group-theoretic structure of CNOT circuits; T14 complements this by providing
an analytic entropy formula for the continuous-angle regime.

\paragraph{Entanglement in noisy stabilizer states.}
Goodenough et al.~\cite{goodenough2024noisy} showed that the spectrum of the
reduced state of a noisy stabilizer state can be expressed in terms of
properties of an associated stabilizer code, connecting entanglement entropy
to syndrome entropy.  This is complementary to our noise model: while
Goodenough et al.\ study Pauli noise on stabilizer states, our IBM hardware
experiments (\cref{sec:implementation}) use a linear noise model
$\VNE_{\mathrm{noisy}} = \eta \cdot \VNE_{\mathrm{ideal}}$ calibrated on
\texttt{ibm\_fez}, with $\eta \approx 0.849$.

\paragraph{Parameterised quantum circuits.}
The broader context of analytic entropy formulas for parameterised circuits
has received growing attention in the variational quantum algorithms
literature~\cite{cerezo2021variational}.  T14 provides an exact closed-form
formula for a specific but natural circuit family, which may be useful as a
benchmark or regulariser in variational settings.

\paragraph{Novelty summary.}
The T14 formula is, to our knowledge, the first closed-form analytic expression
for the von Neumann entropy of a CNOT circuit with continuous rotation angles.
The rank formula (T-OPT) is a known special case; the continuous interpolation
is new.

\section{Preliminaries}
\label{sec:prelim}

\subsection{Notation}

We write $[n] = \{0,1,\dotsc,n-1\}$.
Qubits are indexed from the most-significant bit.
$\ket{x}$ for $x \in \{0,1\}^n$ denotes a computational-basis state.
$\F_2 = \{0,1\}$ is the field with two elements; arithmetic is mod~2.
For a matrix $M \in \F_2^{n_B \times n_A}$ we write $\rk(M)$ for its
rank over $\F_2$.

\subsection{Circuit setup}

Let $n_A, n_B \geq 1$.  We consider a bipartite system $A \otimes B$ with
$n_A + n_B$ qubits total, prepared in

\begin{equation}
  \ket{\psi_{\mathrm{in}}} \;=\;
  \Bigl(\bigotimes_{i=0}^{n_A-1} R_x(\theta_i)\ket{0}\Bigr)
  \;\otimes\;
  \ket{0}^{\otimes n_B},
  \label{eq:product_input}
\end{equation}

where $R_x(\theta)\ket{0} = \cos(\theta/2)\ket{0} - i\sin(\theta/2)\ket{1}$.

A \emph{unidirectional} CNOT layer is then applied: for each pair $(i,j)$ with
$M_{j,i}=1$, the gate $\mathrm{CNOT}(A_i \to B_j)$ is applied.  All gates go
from $A$ to $B$; no gate modifies any $A$-qubit.  The output state is

\begin{equation}
  \ket{\psi} \;=\; U_M \,\ket{\psi_{\mathrm{in}}},
  \qquad
  U_M = \prod_{j,i:\, M_{j,i}=1} \mathrm{CNOT}(A_i,\, n_A + j).
  \label{eq:circuit}
\end{equation}

We study the von Neumann entropy of subsystem $B$:
\begin{equation}
  \VNE(B) \;=\; -\tr[\rho_B \log_2 \rho_B],
  \qquad \rho_B = \tr_A[\ketbra{\psi}{\psi}].
\end{equation}

\section{The Walsh--Hadamard Formula (Theorem T14)}
\label{sec:t14}

\begin{theorem}[T14 --- Walsh--Hadamard entropy formula]
\label{thm:t14}
Let $\ket{\psi}$ be the output of circuit~\eqref{eq:circuit}.  Define,
for each $b \in \{0,1\}^{n_B}$,

\begin{equation}
  p_b \;=\; \frac{1}{2^{n_B}}
            \sum_{s \in \{0,1\}^{n_B}}
            (-1)^{b \cdot s}
            \varphi(s),
  \label{eq:t14_probs}
\end{equation}

where

\begin{equation}
  \varphi(s) \;=\; \prod_{i \,:\, (M^{\top}s)_i = 1} \cos\theta_i.
  \label{eq:phi}
\end{equation}

Then $p_b \geq 0$, $\sum_b p_b = 1$, and

\begin{equation}
  \boxed{\VNE(B) \;=\; \Hbin\!\bigl(\{p_b\}_{b \in \{0,1\}^{n_B}}\bigr).}
  \label{eq:t14}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Since all CNOT gates go from $A$ to $B$, the $A$-qubits are never modified
by $U_M$.  Each computational-basis component $\ket{x}_A$ of the input maps
deterministically to $\ket{Mx \bmod 2}_B$, so the output state is
\[
  \ket{\psi} = \sum_{x \in \{0,1\}^{n_A}} \alpha_x \ket{x}_A \ket{Mx \bmod 2}_B,
\]
where $\alpha_x = \prod_{i=0}^{n_A-1}[(\cos\tfrac{\theta_i}{2})^{1-x_i}(-i\sin\tfrac{\theta_i}{2})^{x_i}]$.

The reduced density matrix $\rho_B$ is diagonal in the computational basis:
\[
  (\rho_B)_{bb} = \sum_{x:\, Mx = b} |\alpha_x|^2.
\]
(Off-diagonal terms $\bra{b}\rho_B\ket{b'}$ with $b \neq b'$ vanish because
they require $\alpha_x \overline{\alpha_{x'}}$ with $Mx = b \neq b' = Mx'$,
and the $A$-basis states $\ket{x}, \ket{x'}$ are orthogonal.)

A Walsh--Hadamard transform over $\{0,1\}^{n_B}$ then converts the diagonal
entries into the form~\eqref{eq:t14_probs}--\eqref{eq:phi}, with $\varphi(s)$
capturing the Fourier characters of the group $\F_2^{n_B}$.  Since all
eigenvalues are non-negative and sum to one, the von Neumann entropy equals
the Shannon entropy of $\{p_b\}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Computational complexity]
\label{rem:complexity}
The Walsh--Hadamard transform makes T14 computable in $O(2^{n_B} \cdot n_B)$
time, which is advantageous when $n_B \ll n_A$.  For balanced partitions
($n_A \approx n_B$), both costs are exponential in the total qubit count and
there is no asymptotic advantage; the formula remains useful as an exact
analytic expression.
\end{remark}

\section{Rank Bound and Optimality}
\label{sec:rank_bound}

\begin{theorem}[T-RANK --- entropy bounded by $\F_2$ rank]
\label{thm:trank}
For all $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^{n_A}$ and all $M \in \F_2^{n_B \times n_A}$,

\begin{equation}
  \VNE(B) \;\leq\; \rk(M).
  \label{eq:trank}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The image $\operatorname{Im}_{\F_2}(M) \subseteq \F_2^{n_B}$ has cardinality
$2^{\rk(M)}$.  Since $\rho_B$ is supported on $\operatorname{Im}(M)$, it has
at most $2^{\rk(M)}$ non-zero eigenvalues, giving
$\VNE(B) \leq \log_2 2^{\rk(M)} = \rk(M)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[T-OPT --- maximum entropy at $\theta = \pi/2$]
\label{thm:topt}
Setting $\theta_i = \pi/2$ for all $i$ yields

\begin{equation}
  \VNE(B)\big|_{\theta = \pi/2} \;=\; \rk(M).
  \label{eq:topt}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
At $\theta_i = \pi/2$, $R_x(\pi/2)\ket{0} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{0} - i\ket{1})$,
so $|\alpha_x|^2 = 2^{-n_A}$ for all $x$.  The eigenvalue associated with
each coset of $\ker_{\F_2}(M)$ is therefore $|\ker|/2^{n_A} = 2^{-\rk(M)}$,
each appearing $2^{\rk(M)}$ times.  Hence $\rho_B$ is the maximally mixed
state on $\operatorname{Im}(M)$ and $\VNE(B) = \rk(M)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
T-OPT recovers the known stabilizer result: at $\theta = \pi/2$ the input
state $R_x(\pi/2)\ket{0} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{0}-i\ket{1})$ is (up to
a global phase) the $-Y$ eigenstate, and the output is a stabilizer state
whose entropy equals the $\F_2$-rank of the generator
matrix~\cite{fattal2004entanglement,audenaert2005entanglement}.
\end{remark}

\section{Multi-Layer Composition (Theorem T15A)}
\label{sec:layers}

\begin{theorem}[T15A --- XOR composition of unidirectional layers]
\label{thm:t15a}
Let $M_1, \dotsc, M_k \in \F_2^{n_B \times n_A}$ be the connectivity
matrices of $k$ sequential \emph{unidirectional} CNOT layers (all gates
directed $A \to B$) applied to the same input state~\eqref{eq:product_input}.
Then

\begin{equation}
  \VNE(B) \;=\; \VNE^{(T14)}\!\bigl(\theta,\; M_{\mathrm{eff}}\bigr),
  \qquad
  M_{\mathrm{eff}} = \bigoplus_{\ell=1}^{k} M_\ell \pmod{2}.
  \label{eq:t15a}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Since all CNOT gates act unidirectionally from $A$ to $B$, the state of
subsystem $A$ is unaffected by the circuit.  Therefore, repeated applications
of the same $\mathrm{CNOT}(A_i \to B_j)$ gate cancel pairwise due to
self-inverseness.  As a result, the overall action of $k$ layers reduces to a
single CNOT layer with connectivity matrix
\[
  M_{\mathrm{eff}} = \bigoplus_{\ell=1}^{k} M_\ell \pmod{2}.
\]
Applying Theorem~\ref{thm:t14} to this effective matrix yields the result.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Unidirectionality is essential]
\label{rem:unidirectional}
T15A requires all layers to be unidirectional ($A \to B$).  If any layer
includes a gate directed $B \to A$, the $A$-qubits are modified and the
XOR reduction no longer holds.  The bidirectional circuit of \cref{sec:bidir}
(T20) illustrates this: the composition $U_{\mathrm{bwd}} \circ U_{\mathrm{fwd}}$
has period 3, not 2, precisely because the backward layer modifies $A$.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[T18 --- period-2 entropy sequence]
\label{cor:t18}
If all layers use the same matrix $M$, the entropy sequence satisfies
$\VNE^{(k)} = \VNE^{(k+2)}$ for all $k \geq 1$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
$M_{\mathrm{eff}}(k) = k \cdot M \bmod 2$, which alternates between $M$
(odd $k$) and $0$ (even $k$).
\end{proof}

\section{Extension to General Pure States (Theorem T17)}
\label{sec:pure}

\begin{theorem}[T17 --- arbitrary pure input on $A$]
\label{thm:t17}
Let $\ket{\psi_A} = \sum_x \alpha_x \ket{x}$ be an arbitrary normalised
pure state on $A$.  After applying $U_M$,

\begin{equation}
  \VNE(B) \;=\; \Hbin\!\Bigl(\bigl\{q_b\bigr\}\Bigr),
  \qquad
  q_b = \sum_{x:\, Mx = b} |\alpha_x|^2.
  \label{eq:t17}
\end{equation}

Moreover, $\VNE(B) = 0$ (i.e.\ $B$ is \emph{blind} to $A$) if and only if
\begin{equation}
  M(x \oplus x') = 0 \quad
  \forall\, x, x' \in \supp(\psi_A).
  \label{eq:blindness}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Since $\ket{\psi_B} = \ket{0}^{\otimes n_B}$ and all gates go $A \to B$,
each computational-basis component $\alpha_x\ket{x}_A$ maps deterministically
to $\ket{Mx}_B$.  The output is $\sum_x \alpha_x \ket{x}_A\ket{Mx}_B$;
tracing out $A$ gives $\rho_B = \sum_b q_b \ketbra{b}{b}$ with $q_b$
as in~\eqref{eq:t17}.

For the blindness criterion: $\VNE(B) = 0$ iff $q_b \in \{0,1\}$ for all $b$,
i.e.\ the map $x \mapsto Mx$ is constant on $\supp(\psi_A)$, which is
equivalent to $M(x \oplus x') = 0$ for all pairs in the support.
\end{proof}

\section{Extension to Mixed Input States (Theorem T-EVRENSEL)}
\label{sec:mixed}

\begin{theorem}[T-EVRENSEL --- diagonal mixed input $\rho_A$]
\label{thm:tevrensel}
If $A$ is in a mixed state $\rho_A = \sum_x d_x \ketbra{x}{x}$ diagonal in
the computational basis, the entropy of $B$ after $U_M$ is

\begin{equation}
  \VNE(B) \;=\; \Hbin\!\Bigl(\bigl\{r_b\bigr\}\Bigr),
  \qquad
  r_b = \sum_{x:\, Mx = b} d_x.
  \label{eq:tevrensel}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By linearity, $\rho_{AB} = \sum_x d_x \, U_M (\ketbra{x}{x} \otimes \ketbra{0}{0}) U_M^\dagger$.
Tracing out $A$ gives $\rho_B = \sum_b r_b \ketbra{b}{b}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
T-EVRENSEL is a direct consequence of the circuit structure: since CNOT gates
only permute computational-basis states, only the diagonal of $\rho_A$
propagates to $B$.  Off-diagonal coherences of $\rho_A$ do not affect $\rho_B$
because the $A$-basis states $\ket{x}$ and $\ket{x'}$ with $x \neq x'$ are
mapped to orthogonal $B$-states (or the same $B$-state, in which case the
cross-term vanishes upon tracing out $A$).  This is immediate from the
computational-basis structure of CNOT and is essentially a restatement of T17
for the case where $\ket{\psi_A}$ is replaced by a classical mixture.
\end{remark}

\section{CZ-Gate Equivalence (CZ-T14)}
\label{sec:cz}

\begin{theorem}[CZ-T14]
\label{thm:czt14}
Replace every CNOT$(A_i \to B_j)$ by a CZ gate, and initialise each $B$-qubit
in $\ket{+} = (\ket{0}+\ket{1})/\sqrt{2}$.  The resulting von Neumann entropy
of $B$ is identical to that of the CNOT circuit with $B$ initialised in
$\ket{0}$:

\begin{equation}
  \VNE^{\mathrm{CZ}}\!(B) \;=\; \VNE^{\mathrm{CNOT}}\!(B).
  \label{eq:czt14}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Since $H\,\mathrm{CNOT}\,H = \mathrm{CZ}$ (on the target qubit), the CZ
circuit with $\ket{+}$ initialisation is locally unitarily equivalent to the
CNOT circuit with $\ket{0}$ initialisation.  Local unitaries on $B$ do not
change $\rho_B$'s spectrum, hence $\VNE(B)$ is preserved.
\end{proof}

\section{Mutual Information and Subadditivity (Theorem T-MI)}
\label{sec:mi}

\begin{theorem}[T-MI --- mutual information non-negativity]
\label{thm:tmi}
For any bipartition $B = B_1 \cup B_2$ (corresponding to a row partition of
$M$ into $M_1, M_2$),

\begin{equation}
  I(B_1 ; B_2) \;=\; \VNE(B_1) + \VNE(B_2) - \VNE(B_1 B_2) \;\geq\; 0.
  \label{eq:tmi}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
This is an immediate consequence of the strong subadditivity of
von Neumann entropy~\cite{lieb1973proof}.
\end{proof}

\section{Bidirectional CNOT and Period-3 Dynamics (Theorem T20)}
\label{sec:bidir}

Consider a symmetric circuit on $2 + 2$ qubits: a \emph{forward} CNOT layer
$U_{\mathrm{fwd}} = \mathrm{CNOT}(A_0,B_0)\,\mathrm{CNOT}(A_1,B_1)$ followed
by a \emph{backward} layer
$U_{\mathrm{bwd}} = \mathrm{CNOT}(B_0,A_0)\,\mathrm{CNOT}(B_1,A_1)$.
Define $U = U_{\mathrm{bwd}}\, U_{\mathrm{fwd}}$.

Note that $U$ is \emph{not} covered by T15A: the backward layer modifies the
$A$-qubits, violating the unidirectionality assumption of \cref{thm:t15a}.

\begin{proposition}[T20 --- period-3 dynamics]
\label{thm:t20}
The operator $U$ satisfies $U^3 = I_{16}$ and generates a cyclic group of
order $3$.  Consequently, the entropy sequence $\VNE^{(k)}(B)$ obeys
$\VNE^{(k)} = \VNE^{(k+3)}$ for all $k \geq 1$, with minimal period $3$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Verification]
Write each CNOT as its $16 \times 16$ permutation matrix.  Explicit matrix
computation gives $U^3 = I_{16}$ to machine precision
($\max|U^3 - I| < 10^{-13}$), and $\max|U^2 - I| > 10^{-6}$, confirming
minimal period 3.  See \cref{tab:results} and the accompanying code.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
We have verified numerically that $U^3 = I_{16}$ to machine precision.
An analytic characterization of this group structure---via the minimal
polynomial of $U$ over $\F_2$, which divides $x^3 - 1 = (x-1)(x^2+x+1)$---
is left for future work.
\end{remark}

\subsection{IBM hardware experiments}

The T14 formula was also tested against IBM \texttt{ibm\_fez} hardware using
quantum state tomography on four circuits (identity, cross, mixed, A0\_both)
at $\theta = \pi/3$.  A linear noise model $\VNE_{\mathrm{noisy}} = \eta \cdot
\VNE_{\mathrm{ideal}}$ fits the data with $\eta \approx 0.849$, consistent
with the gate error rates of the \texttt{ibm\_fez} device.  This provides
experimental evidence that T14 correctly predicts the ideal entropy, and that
hardware noise introduces a multiplicative attenuation.

\section{Discussion and Outlook}
\label{sec:discussion}

The QSE framework provides exact, efficiently computable entropy formulas for
an important class of quantum circuits.  Several extensions are natural:

\paragraph{Parameterised circuits.}
The T14 formula is differentiable in $\theta$, making it directly applicable
to variational quantum algorithms and quantum machine learning where entropy
is used as a regulariser or objective~\cite{cerezo2021variational}.

\paragraph{Non-product inputs.}
\cref{thm:t17} generalises T14 to arbitrary pure states on $A$; a further
extension to entangled inputs on $B$ would require a different approach, as
the diagonal structure of $\rho_B$ relies on $B$ starting in $\ket{0}$.

\paragraph{Clifford extensions.}
Adding Hadamard and phase gates changes the stabiliser group but preserves
the $\F_2$ linear structure.  A unified Walsh-spectrum formula for general
Clifford circuits is an open problem.  The gap between the present circuit
family and general Clifford circuits is substantial: any gate on $B$ before
the CNOT layer, or any $B \to A$ gate, breaks the unidirectionality assumption.

\paragraph{Analytic proof of T20.}
As noted in \cref{sec:bidir}, an algebraic proof that $U^3 = I$ for the
bidirectional circuit---via the minimal polynomial of $U$ over $\F_2$---would
strengthen the result from a numerically verified proposition to a theorem.

\paragraph{Tensor-network perspective.}
The period-3 result (T20) suggests a non-trivial group-theoretic structure
in the causal cone of the circuit.  Understanding its tensor-network
representation may yield efficient classical simulation algorithms.
